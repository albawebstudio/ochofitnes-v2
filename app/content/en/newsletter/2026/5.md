## This month Question:

**Low back pain:**

1.  What can cause it?
2.  Can it get better?
3.  What’s the recipe?

If you’ve ever injured your low back, you know it’s a horrible feeling. It can be debilitating. It can range from feeling stiff to excruciating to move in any direction. It can force you to completely stop all activities. When I first started my health journey, I worked at a chiropractor’s office in Monterey, CA. The main reason for visits? Back pain. I learned that our amazing body, to protect our spine, will first cause our back muscles to spasm to prevent more injury. It’s like a warning sign, but then the injury can get worse and lead to more serious problems if we ignore the signs. The trauma can come from picking something up wrong, pulling something very hard in an awkward position, a fall, bad exercise, muscle imbalance/weakness, or sitting too much!
   
**Can you make your back injury better?** I truly believe so. I have witnessed people, including myself, recover. It 
takes time but most important a plan. Once we understand what our condition is ex: disk herniation, arthritis, surgery, sprain/strain, then we can then plan what movements to limit. Then we can also start corrective exercises, therapy, and massages to prevent further damage and reoccurring injuries. I have been purposely training my low back to bring you guys better feedback. Two months of specific back exercises for each workout and my back is the strongest ever. Strengthening my low and erector spinae muscles has made every exercise stronger. My low back feels stable and healthy. 

**These are some of the best exercises recommended for low-back strength:**

**Trunk extensions * Romanian deadlifts * Core exercises * Good mornings * glute/hamstring * reverse hypers * snatch 
deadlifts**

## Tip of the month:

**Are we mentally tough? Or do we give up too easily?**

I truly believe that we are stronger than we can ever imagine and able to do
more than what our mind thinks. We are capable of more mentally and
physically.

The first time I truly learned and applied the idea of mental toughness was
after reading the book **“Can’t Hurt Me”** by David Goggins, an ex-navy seal and
army ranger. It’s straightforward to talk about being something, but I love people who
walk the walk. At times, we limit ourselves because of age, past failures, or
injuries. In the book I learned that our mind will stop us way before our body
actually gives up. There are many lessons that we can apply from his book that
actually work, but the one that made my mind tougher was 100 pullups. That’s
right. David said as long as it takes but complete the pullups. I was only able
to 6 or 7 before, how in the world was I going to do 100! I went into the garage
and did 5! Then a few more. The first thing my mind said was “dude it’s
impossible” but I kept hearing David’s voice “be a savage”. My muscles could
literally not pick me up anymore, so before I would have stopped, but I told my
mind I wasn’t going anywhere. I did 100 pullups that night. It took close to 1
hour and a half. I was sore for days. This later became my warmup. It
eventually started taking 15–20 minutes to complete 100 pullups. My mind
and body had adapted. I now apply this to running, the air bike, or anything that
seems impossible/uncomfortable now. It works. My kids know it works, and
now science backs it all up.

Don’t live life scared because of your age, injuries, or people’s opinions on
what you should be doing. Be a savage.

### Have you done an exercise that felt difficult, but your coach thought otherwise?

**When something feels heavy:** remember Newton’s second law — Force.

**Mass x acceleration = F**

I like to look at the speed of the bar/dumbbells to try to access the “truer”
difficulty of an exercise. If the speed of the weight is moving relatively fast, it’s a
very strong indicator the weight isn’t that heavy, but our mind, because of experiences, can literally make us think otherwise. It’s our minds that set the
first boundary. Trust the process team. We are stronger than we think.
